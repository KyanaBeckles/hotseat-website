Litigation Animation Examples · Trial Presentation Case Studies

Three cases. Three ways a jury needed to see it.

Every animation below is built from the record — depositions, site data, and expert input — and rendered for the one audience that matters: jurors who've never seen the inside of a machine, an intersection, or a refinery before.

Accident Reconstruction

The legal team had an accident outside a Florida beachside nightclub, told from every angle but one that actually explained it: witness depositions with conflicting recollections, an overhead map, Google Street View imagery, and a handful of client photographs — none of it, on its own, forming a ready-made visual story a jury could follow.

The animation rebuilt the scene in 3DS Max from that record, tying every account to a fixed point on the map so the geography stayed consistent no matter whose testimony was driving a given moment. V-Ray rendering and After Effects compositing turned the reconstruction into a single continuous sequence rather than a stitched-together set of exhibits.

In front of a jury, that consistency is the whole point: once viewers have one spatial reference for the location, every witness's account plugs into the same mental map instead of competing with it — which is what lets a jury actually resolve conflicting testimony instead of getting lost in it.

Built with: 3DS Max, V-Ray, After Effects

Formax Patty Machine

This was a patent dispute over how a machine actually worked. Formax needed to show a judge and jury with no mechanical background exactly how its patty machine's rotating-arm mechanism operated, and how that operation differed from a competing design — the kind of distinction that decides the case but is nearly impossible to convey in testimony alone.

The mechanism was modeled in Rhino3D for precision and brought into 3DS Max for animation, rendered in V-Ray, and finished in After Effects. Clarity, timing, and mechanical accuracy took priority over spectacle at every step — the arm had to move exactly as the real machine moved, at a pace a courtroom could follow.

The result is a working model a non-technical audience can watch and understand on one viewing: how the rotating arm cycles, what it does at each stage, and why that differs from the competing mechanism at the center of the dispute.

Built with: Rhino3D, 3DS Max, V-Ray, After Effects

Ground Contamination

An environmental litigation matter involving a refinery on St. Croix needed to explain something that happens entirely underground and over time: how contamination spread beneath the site, where remediation wells were installed, and how the contaminated material was reduced as remediation proceeded.

The visualization was built from ArcGIS site data, LIDAR modeling, and well-placement records — grounding the underground scene in the same technical data the experts relied on, rather than an illustrator's approximation. 3DS Max handled the modeling, V-Ray the rendering, and After Effects the compositing that turned site data into a clear visual sequence.

For a courtroom, that matters because contamination and remediation are invisible processes by nature. Turning expert-grounded site data into something jurors can watch happen — spread, wells, reduction — is what makes an expert's technical opinion legible to people who will never see the site itself.

Built with: 3DS Max, V-Ray, After Effects (ArcGIS + LIDAR site data)
