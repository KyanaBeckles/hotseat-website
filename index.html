What is a trial "hot seat"?

The hot seat is the technology chair at counsel's table. The operator sitting in it runs everything the jury sees: exhibits displayed on demand, documents highlighted line-by-line, deposition clips played at the exact designation, and animations cued to expert testimony — in real time, under courtroom pressure, without stalling your examination. Hotseat combines that operator role with the studio that builds the visuals themselves.

Questions legal teams ask

What does a hot seat operator actually do during trial?

They sit with counsel and drive the courtroom technology in real time — pulling up exhibits on request, highlighting testimony, playing synced deposition video, and cueing demonstratives so the examination never loses momentum in front of the jury.

Why use animation instead of talking a jury through it?

Jurors retain visuals dramatically better than verbal description. One accurate animation of how the machine rotated, how the vehicle traveled, or how the contamination spread replaces an hour of confusing testimony — and stays in the jury room memory.

Will the animation survive an admissibility challenge?

Demonstratives are routinely admitted when they fairly and accurately illustrate evidence already in the record. That's why every frame is grounded in depositions, photographs, maps, and expert opinion — accuracy and a defensible basis are the whole design brief.

When should we bring you in?

When exhibit lists and the pretrial order start taking shape. Visuals built weeks out survive objections better and cost less than rush work the weekend before openings.
